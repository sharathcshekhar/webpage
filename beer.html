When I am not doing my research and out of beer, I am found brewing my own. I love beer and I have camouflaged my beer addiction as a hobby through homebrewing. I am a nanoscale brewer, brewing beers in batches of 1-2 gallons. I mostly use malt extracts and use partial mashing only when required. Although, I have used partial mashing for every beer I have brewed of late. I use dry yeast for all my beers and they turn out to be great. I like experimenting with my beers and don't pay much attention to the BJCP style guidelines, as long as I get a great tasting beer. That explains why I decided to brew an Orange Hef! If you live in the Buffalo area and want to get in touch to start brewing, I will be glad to help!

Russian Imperial Stout (KBS Clone)

This started as a clone of the famous KBS beer. I didn't have some of the grains the original recipe called for. So, I made a few substitutions to create a similar, but different Imperial Stout, infused with lots of chocolate and coffee, aged with bourbon soaked oak cubes. This beer is now sitting in secondary and I am expecting an ABV of around 10%. After transferring to the secondary, I bottle conditioned the extra beer for a couple of weeks and tasted them. Like the original KBS, it has deep coffee and chocolate flavors, but the body is much thinner (maybe because of dry yeast). If I have to brew this again, I will add a little more flaked oats and two-row malt and mash it at much lower temperatures.

Vanilla Latte Stout

Here is a coffee milk stout with vanilla additions. It is a delight for coffee lovers. A word of caution before brewing any milk stouts: most of the brewing softwares treat lactose as a fermentable while estimating the FG and ABV. To get the desired ABV, you have to add more base malt. I did the same mistake, and the beer was watered down. If I have to brew it again, I will try to hit OG of 1.060, which will still keep the ABV around 5.

Honey Ginger Ale

Summer ale brewed with fresh ginger and honey malt. With intense flavor of ginger and finishes with a hint of honey, this makes a great summer ale. I used both, wildflower honey as a late addition, and honey malt to get the honey flavors. The recommended way to add honey is to add it to the secondary after a complex pasteurization procedure. Maybe I will try that someday! I used Muntons dry yeast for this and the beer turned out to be very cloudy. I wish I had chosen a Safale-04 or 05 instead.

Honey Orange Wheat Ale

Here is an American wheat beer with brewed with lots of orange and honey. Another refreshing summer ale. I added sweet orange peel to boil, orange zest, and the fruit to the secondary. The result was a pleasant citrusy orange beer. Although I brewed this as an extract beer, I did manage to steep the honey malt at 155F. If I had to do this again, I will partial mash the honey malt with a base malt to get more honey character.
