When I am not doing my research and out of beer, I am found brewing my own. I love beer and I have camouflaged my beer addiction as a hobby through homebrewing. I am a nanoscale brewer, brewing beers in batches of 1-2 gallons. I mostly use malt extracts and use partial mashing only when required. Although, I have used partial mashing for every beer I have brewed of late. I use dry yeast for all my beers and they turn out to be great. I like experimenting with my beers and don't pay much attention to the BJCP style guidelines, as long as I get a great tasting beer. That explains why I decided to brew an Orange Hef!
Recently, I entered my first homebrew competition, Amber Wave of Grains with a Belgian Dubbel, under BJCP category 26B. Although my entry did not win a medal, it was up against some of the best brewers in the Westren New York Area and was received very well by the judges! It was rated "Very Goood", scoring a 33.5 on 50, just below "Excellent" (38 and above; beers with this rating will bag a prize in most competitions), and "Outstanding" (world class beers).
If you live in the Buffalo area and want to get in touch to start brewing, I will be glad to help!

German Red Ale (Altbier)

An Altbier is fermented at ale temperatures but conditioned at lager temperatures. I do not have any lagering capabilities and my fridge is not big enough to hold my secondaries. Nonetheless, I attempted brewing this Altbier and lagered the bottled beer. The result—a delicious caramelly red ale with sweetness powered by Munich malt.
I made a small experiment with this batch. A third of the bottles was lagered for three weeks, and then, let them sit in room temperature for carbonation. The second third of the bottles, was carbonated at room temperature and then lagered for three weeks. The last third of the bottles were in room temperate for six weeks and chilled just before drinking. I will update the results of blind-tasting the three variations of the beer.

Dubbel Trouble

A Trappist style Belgian Dubbel. I brewed this with home made Belgian Dark Candy. Copper red in color, extremely aromatic infused with flavors from dark caramel and dry fruit. I entered Amber Wave of Grains with this beer and received very favorable reviews by the judges. The results can be seen here. As the judges have pointed out, the beer was thinner for a Dubbel and could have been more maltier. The next time I brew this, I would add some Munich and aromatic malts and perhaps increase my mash temperatures. Otherwise, this was a perfect batch.

Russian Imperial Stout (KBS Clone)

This started as a clone of the famous KBS beer. I didn't have some of the grains the original recipe called for. So, I made a few substitutions to create a similar, but different Imperial Stout, infused with lots of chocolate and coffee, aged with bourbon soaked oak cubes. This beer is now sitting in secondary and I am expecting an ABV of around 10%. After transferring to the secondary, I bottle conditioned the extra beer for a couple of weeks and tasted them. Like the original KBS, it has deep coffee and chocolate flavors, but the body is much thinner (maybe because of dry yeast). If I have to brew this again, I will add a little more flaked oats and two-row malt and mash it at much lower temperatures.

Vanilla Latte Stout

Here is a coffee milk stout with vanilla additions. It is a delight for coffee lovers. A word of caution before brewing any milk stouts: most of the brewing softwares treat lactose as a fermentable while estimating the FG and ABV. To get the desired ABV, you have to add more base malt. I did the same mistake, and the beer was watered down. If I have to brew it again, I will try to hit OG of 1.060, which will still keep the ABV around 5.

Honey Ginger Ale

Summer ale brewed with fresh ginger and honey malt. With intense flavor of ginger and finishes with a hint of honey, this makes a great summer ale. I used both, wildflower honey as a late addition, and honey malt to get the honey flavors. The recommended way to add honey is to add it to the secondary after a complex pasteurization procedure. Maybe I will try that someday! I used Muntons dry yeast for this and the beer turned out to be very cloudy. I wish I had chosen a Safale-04 or 05 instead.

Honey Orange Wheat Ale

Here is an American wheat beer with brewed with lots of orange and honey. Another refreshing summer ale. I added sweet orange peel to boil, orange zest, and the fruit to the secondary. The result was a pleasant citrusy orange beer. Although I brewed this as an extract beer, I did manage to steep the honey malt at 155F. If I had to do this again, I will partial mash the honey malt with a base malt to get more honey character.
